Policy

While car crashes remain a leading cause of death, roads are getting safer. Over the last half-century, the number of automobile fatalities per vehicle mile traveled in the United States decreased 80 percent, from 5.3 deaths per 100 million vehicle mile traveled in 1965 to 1.07 today.

Policy changes have played a large role in reducing the number of fatalities. Until the 1960s, for instance, there were no U.S. seat belt laws. Today, 49 of the 50 states require passengers to wear seat belts. Explore below how average fatalities differ depending on statesâ€™ policies.

The majority of states ban texting while driving, but it's not yet illegal in four states. Research shows that anti-texting laws are associated with a statistically significant drop in traffic fatalities, however this effect is most pronounced in young populations.

A 2014 study by researchers at the University of Alabama at Birmingham found that primary texting laws were associated with a 5 percent drop in fatalities among drivers between the ages of 15 and 21, but there was no statistically significant effect among older drivers.

Most states don't ban handheld devices, which means drivers are allowed to talk on the phone while driving.

Despite the clear correlation between bans on handheld devices and lower automobile fatality rates, some studies have disputed the bans' effectiveness, while others reaffirm the importance of banning handheld devices. One study by University of Utah researchers found that using a handheld device while driving is just as dangerous and deadly as drinking while driving.

Every state with the exception of New Hampshire has some type of seatbelt law. In states with primary seatbelt laws, police can pull drivers over for not wearing their seatbelts. A 1995 study by the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration showed that fatality rates dropped 21 percent in five states that began primary enforcement of seatbelt laws.

In most states, drivers who get punished for drinking and driving get their license suspended for a time period, but there remain six states in which there is no such punishment. The average number of fatalities per vehicle mile traveled is slightly lower in states with longer license suspension periods for first-time DUI offenders.

Speed limits in rural areas differ by state. Higher rural speed limits are associated with more automobile fatalities.

A recent study by the Insurance Institute for Highway Safety showed that increases in speed limits over the last 20 years have led to 33,000 additional deaths. In 2013 alone, speed limit increases led to an additional 1,900 Americans losing their lives.

Most states don't permit open containers of alcohol in a car, but nine states still allow the practice. Open container bans are correlated with lower fatality rates in the states that have implemented them.

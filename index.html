The car crash is a cacophonous demonstration of the unfortunate convergence of time, place, and human imperfection. In 2014, more than 32,000 people died in automobile accidents in the United States -- ranking traffic fatalities among the leading causes of death for Americans.

With these visualizations, we hope to illuminate trends in the times, locations, and causes of these all-too-frequent tragedies. Our look into the policies that affect the frequency of automobile fatalities provides a lens into what additional policies can be implemented to save lives.

Changing seasons often inspire holiday memories, visions of renewed foliage, or ideas for revamped color palettes. Interestingly, the number of fatal highway accidents also depends on the time of year. Every year, the number of fatal crashes drops dramatically over the winter months, hitting a low point around February. Then, reliably, fatal crashes surge again and peak around July. Alcohol

In the United States, someone dies from a crash involving an alcohol-impaired driver every 53 minutes. Though often associated with festivity and fruitfulness, alcohol can be a serious threat to the lives of drivers and passengers on the roads. Policy

While car crashes remain a leading cause of death, roads are getting safer. Over the last half-century, the number of automobile fatalities per vehicle mile traveled in the United States decreased 80 percent. Policy changes have played a large role in reducing the number of fatalities. Until the 1960s, for instance, there were no U.S. seat belt laws. Today, 49 of the 50 states require passengers to wear seat belts. Explore below how average fatalities differ depending on states’ policies.

The majority of states ban texting while driving, but it's not yet illegal in four states. Research shows that anti-texting laws are associated with a statistically significant drop in traffic fatalities, however this effect is most pronounced in young populations.


A 2014 study by researchers at the University of Alabama at Birmingham found that primary texting laws were associated with a 5 percent drop in fatalities among drivers between the ages of 15 and 21, but there was no statistically significant effect among older drivers.

Most states don't ban handheld devices, which means drivers are allowed to talk on the phone while driving.

Despite the clear correlation between bans on handheld devices and lower automobile fatality rates, some studies have disputed the bans' effectiveness, while others reaffirm the importance of banning handheld devices. One study by University of Utah researchers found that using a handheld device while driving is just as dangerous and deadly as drinking while driving.

Every state with the exception of New Hampshire has some type of seatbelt law. In states with primary seatbelt laws, police can pull drivers over for not wearing their seatbelts. In states with secondary seatbelt laws, police can only issue punishments for drivers not using seatbelts if the driver has been pulled over for some other reason. Research shows that blah blah.

A 1995 study by the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration showed that fatality rates dropped 21 percent in five states that began primary enforcement of seatbelt laws.

In most states, drivers who get punished for drinking and driving get their license suspended for a time period, but there remain six states in which there is no such punishment. The average number of fatalities per vehicle mile traveled is slightly lower in states with longer license suspension periods for first-time DUI offenders.

Speed limits in rural areas differ by state. Higher rural speed limits are associated with more automobile fatalities.

A recent study by the Insurance Institute for Highway Safety showed that increases in speed limits over the last 20 years have led to 33,000 additional deaths. In 2013 alone, speed limit increases led to an additional 1,900 Americans losing their lives.

Most states don't permit open containers of alcohol in a car, but nine states still allow the practice. Open container bans are correlated with lower fatality rates in the states that have implemented them.
